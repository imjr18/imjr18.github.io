## Problem

Ride-sharing drivers optimizing purely for their own fare produce a system that is locally rational and globally poor — long detours, idle vehicles clustered in the wrong places, unnecessary emissions. This project asks the CS-meets-Economics question directly: **if agents carried an explicit incentive to help the network, could selfish learners converge on a fairer, more efficient equilibrium?**

## Approach

The fleet is trained with **MADDPG** — a centralized critic that sees the joint state and actions during training, and decentralized actors that act on local observations at deployment.

The mechanism-design piece is an **altruism score** capturing driver–rider reciprocity, folded into reward shaping:

$$
R = R_{\text{individual}} + \lambda \cdot R_{\text{altruism}}
$$

so each agent's return blends its own fare with a network-benefit term weighted by $\lambda$. Training happens under genuine **non-stationarity**: the driver and rider populations turn over stochastically, so the environment every agent faces keeps shifting as the others learn. The system is A/B-tested in simulation against greedy, profit-driven baselines.

## Results

Against the greedy baseline the altruistic fleet shows **higher utilization, fewer detours, lower CO₂, and more stable long-run equilibria**, with the largest gaps under volatile demand.

These are **directional, simulation-based** results — the framing throughout is A/B comparison in a simulator, not a deployed system, and the gains are reported qualitatively because that is what the experiments support.

## Rigor notes / what I learned

- Reward shaping is mechanism design: the altruism term is a designed incentive, and the interesting result is that a small $\lambda$ changes the *equilibrium*, not just individual behavior.
- Non-stationarity from population turnover is the hard part; a centralized critic is what makes the actors trainable through it.
- The project is deliberately the bridge between the two halves of the degree — the RL is CS, the equilibrium and incentive analysis is economics.
