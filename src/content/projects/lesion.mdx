## Problem

Segmenting stroke lesions in CT is hard, but the harder problem this project actually confronted was **my own evaluation**. An early model looked excellent. It wasn't — and the story of finding out is the point.

## Approach

The work is a documented arc, v1 → v5, and the most important step is the one where the number got *worse*.

### The leak

The v1 split was slice-level: individual CT slices were shuffled and divided into train and validation. But adjacent slices from the *same patient* are nearly identical, so slices the model trained on had near-twins in the validation set. The reported 89% Dice was measuring memorization, not generalization.

<Figure id="2" caption="SLICE-LEVEL vs PATIENT-LEVEL SPLITTING">
  <SplitDiagram />
</Figure>

Re-splitting at the **patient level** — every slice from a patient lands entirely in train or entirely in validation — dropped held-out Dice to an honest **43–53%**. That drop is the project working correctly.

### The champion

The v3 model is **UNet++** trained with **Focal Tversky loss**, which lets you weight false positives and false negatives asymmetrically — useful when lesions are small and recall matters. The Tversky index and its focal form:

$$
\text{TI} = \frac{TP}{TP + \alpha\,FP + \beta\,FN}
\qquad
\mathcal{L}_{FT} = (1 - \text{TI})^{\gamma}
$$

v4 swept $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ and patient-aware augmentation; v5 tried a Swin-UNet transformer hybrid that came out comparable rather than clearly better.

## Results

The champion reaches **51.7% held-out Dice** and **62.5% recall** on a genuinely held-out patient set. Every later experiment is reported against that same clean protocol.

## Rigor notes / what I learned

- Slice-level vs patient-level splitting is the difference between a flattering lie and a usable number.
- The audit that halves your headline metric is the most valuable result in the project.

> I'd rather report a real 51.7% than a fake 90%.
